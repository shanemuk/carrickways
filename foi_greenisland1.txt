## YES  
Buckle up - this is long! I *very strongly* wish to see safe reliable access to our shore restored to the families and ratepayers of Greenisland. Here are some reasons why I and dozens of my neighbours have been fighting for this blockade for years, and why we won't give up: 
1. HEALTH & RECREATION Outdoor exercise is of proven benefit to physical and mental health. Walking and paddling on our beach is the perfect antidote to stress, and allows us to fill up our lungs with fresh sea air, while getting as energetic (or not) as we wish. Kids can play and learn in a safe beautiful environment that is available to all ages and abilities, provided the tide and the weather are right. 
2. SAFETY At present, all access points to and from the beach are blocked along a distance of over 2km from Shorelands ("The Gut") to Torytown (Trooperslane). There are several tidal pinchpoints along this stretch, meaning that people who start out walking from one direction risk being cut off by the tide. This is an unacceptable situation, and in addition to the basic public safety aspects, places the Council in a position of liability. An access point close to Jointure Bay or Island Park would considerably mitigate this risk, as well as permit regular safety assessments by Council personnel. Overall, however, it's a very safe stretch of shoreline. 
3. ENVIRONMENT A recent evidence-based Habitat Regulations Assessment carried out for the Department of Infrastructure, looking at potential impact on birds on the Green Island concluded that there would not be any adverse environmental impact of opening access. We argue that, on the contrary, access would be highly beneficial, since residents have an interest in protecting their environment, and would be motivated to organise beach clean-ups and other activities that will preserve, protect and enhance the shore, without generally incurring any cost to Council. It's a win-win. 
4. COMMUNITY As a community, we want to make Greenisland somewhere we can be proud of, whether we live on the Shore Road, the Top Road or off the Station Road. The beach is a perfect place for people to see each other in a relaxed, informal surrounding, and really appreciate where we live. Access helps us look out for our neighbours, and considerably reduce the risks of crime or antisocial behaviour. But it's more than that. The Green Island gives our village its name - it is by definition an indispensable part of our townscape character, and the shore should be accessible to everyone. Just because it's accessible to everyone, that doesn't mean everyone is going to be on the beach all the time. One of the great things about the beach is that it is so quiet and relatively undisturbed. Even with secured access, however, it is very unlikely that large numbers of people will visit at any one time, so the prospect of neighbours being inconvenienced by big crowds is very remote indeed, and cannot be considered a valid objection. 
5. RIGHTS There is also the issue of basic rights. This beach is owned by the Council between the high and low mean tidal reaches from Shorelands to Kilroot. We the residents are ratepayers to MEABC. It is a violation both of our rights under the Access to the Countryside Order 1983 and our general rights as ratepayers that some residents who happen to live adjacent to the shore have unfettered access to Council property, while families who live just metres away (and who used to have access before rampant development, increased traffic and gating-off effectively removed their access) don't. It is time that all residents (and Council staff) had their rightful access restored. 
CONCLUSION The Council-owned beach is a fantastic asset to MEABC. We love it, we want to care for it and enjoy it, and we're fed up having to climb gates and trespass to get onto it. There is an opportunity here to right a historic wrong and get real value from this natural asset. 

I anticipate some residents, who live on the shore side, and currently enjoy unfettered access to Council property, will be happy with the status quo, and would not like to share the shore. They are likely to raise some issues which the other residents and families would strongly rebut. Here are a few - there may be others - but the proclivities and perceived privileges of the few should not be allowed to overrule the wellbeing, rights and the arguments of the many. 
1. Antisocial behaviour, noise & crime: if we open access, thugs and louts will loiter, congregate, make noise, damage private, council & NIW property, and potentially commit crimes. RESPONSE: There is ZERO evidence of this being the case right now (when antisocial elements could easily jump fences etc), and if anything, local residents having access means that we can look out for each other and report antisocial behaviour, so this argument is not only weak, but the counter-argument is much stronger. Eyes on the beach will reduce the risks. (It could be argued that blocking access is the true antisocial behaviour here). 
2. Safety: slippery rocks, tidal pinch points, wash from the ferries, pollution etc put people at risk: RESPONSE: opening access means people can get FROM the beach as well as TO it, meaning they are much less likely to get cut off. It also means that in an emergency, it's much easier to alert the authorities and for help to be delivered on the beach. Pollution events will be detected *earlier* and therefore mitigated. So this counter-argument is invalid. Indeed, if it were considered valid, the logic would be to close off the entire shore of Northern Ireland, which is clearly bonkers. 
3. Environmental damage from lots of people accessing the beach: RESPONSE: The Habitat Regulations Assessment referenced above https://github.com/shanemuk/carrickways/blob/main/Gre
en_Island_Belfast_Lough_HRA_August_2021_Redacted.pdf concluded that there would NOT be any significant negative impact of opening access. As mentioned above, residents are motivated to keep our (*our*) Council beach clean. 
4. Property valuation would fall: RESPONSE: It is not credible that property valuation would be adversely affected in any way. This is a non-argument. 
5. Parking & traffic would increase: RESPONSE: Anyone driving a car will most likely proceed to Loughshore, where there is good parking available, along with other amenities. It is not credible that opening access will result in a large increase in traffic. Any illegal parking can be dealt with in line with existing legislation and processes. 
6. There is already access at Shorelands ("The Gut") and Trooperslane/Torytown: RESPONSE - there is >2km between these points, and the area of beach around Jointure Bay and the Green Island is only accessible at very low tide from these directions, for a very short period. This is NOT adequate, and CANNOT be considered a valid argument against restoring access at Island Park/Jointure Bay. 
CONCLUSION: The above potential counter-arguments are completely refuted, and we the residents and ratepayers would be happy to analyse any additional "no" arguments, and are pretty certain we can refute them also. It should NOT be accepted as default that access be denied for 2km of beach - there MUST be a strong positive case in order to overrule the rights of the majority of families who live in Greenisland and along the Shore Road.
I have placed some material on a Github web page here: https://github.com/shanemuk/carrickwa
ys/blob/main/meabc_sharetheshore.md I have also been posting a fair bit on multiple social media platforms using the#ShareTheShore hashtag. We have a potential win-win here - let's make this happen. 
The above is quite impassioned, and I hope it communicates the strength of feeling around this - I have neighbours talking to me every day about this. We, the families who live along the Shore Road, are incredibly grateful to the Councillors, Officers and MLAs from many different political perspectives who have supported us over the years and who share our desire to benefit the people and townscape of Greenisland. 
I am very happy to address Council or participate in any other events in relation to this - contact details above.

If the NIW laneway were to be opened for general public access, I could walk onto the beach 150m away. Whereas, a return journey to Jointure Bay via "The Gut" or opposite Trooperslane, would involve a 3 mile round trip. This journey would also involve negotiating significant obstacles such as lengths of broken pipework and slippery rocky outcrops. It would also involve exposure to the risk of being cut-off by an incoming tide.
Council have long-recognised the need for general public access to the Jointure Bay region: 
- As far back as 1988, Carrickfergus Council recommended that a laneway at 87a Shore Road be asserted as a PROW. However, as Council never fully completed the assertion process (i.e. the PROW was never registered with the Land & Property Service) this PROW was never established. 
- In a Council-supported "Greenisland Development Framework 2015" consultation, "weakness number 8" identified "limited access to the coast" in the Jointure Bay region. 
Furthermore, despite: 
- Council recognising the above need for general public access 
- Council's awareness that public access to Jointure Bay aligned with their own Health & Wellbeing Policy 
- Council's awareness, over the past 5 years, of a widely-supported, local "Share the Shore" campaign seeking access to the Jointure Bay region 
- Council commissioning a Habitat Risk Assessment, which concluded that increased public footfall at Jointure Bay (if general public access was granted) would have no impact on any of the wildlife species listed as using the area 
- Council possessing "Compulsory Powers for Creation", under Article 12 of the "Access to the Countryside (NI) Order 1983", to create a PROW 
Council have, to date, failed to create/provide general public access to the Jointure Bay region. 
NOTE: For many years, prior to the "Share the Shore" campaign, general public access to the beach at Jointure Bay was available via the direlect site at 103 Shore Road (before this site was sold and access closed off). This route involved the use of part of the NIW laneway to gain beach access. During this lengthy period of access, there was no anti-social behaviour, or indeed any objections from shore-side residents or NIW. 
Lastly, NIW have for years operated (and continue to operate) a discriminatory practice of permitting / tolerating access to their laneway by lane-side residents, whilst denying access by the general public. This disgraceful inequality of access / maladministration must be terminated, at long last, by Council taking action to open the laneway.

## YES  
Outdoor exercise is of proven benefit to physical and mental health. Walking and paddling on our beach is the perfect antidote to stress, and allows us to fill up our lungs with fresh sea air, while getting as energetic (or not) as we wish. It's available to all ages and abilities, provided the tide and the weather are right. 
At present, all access points to and from the beach are blocked along a distance of over 2km from Shorelands ("The Gut") to Torytown (Trooperslane). There are several tidal pinchpoints along this stretch, meaning that people who start out walking from one direction risk being cut off by the tide. This is an unacceptable situation, and in addition to the basic public safety aspects, places the Council in a position of liability, should anything happen to anyone venturing along the beach who is not familiar with the tides. An access point close to Jointure Bay or Island Park would considerably mitigate this risk, as well as permit regular safety assessments by Council personnel. 
It is not at all likely that re-opening access to the beach will cause a large influx of people. A recent Habitat Regulations Assessment carried out for the Department of Infrastructure, looking at potential impact on birds on the Green Island concluded that there would not be any adverse environmental impact of opening access. We would argue that, on the contrary, opening access would be beneficial, since residents have an interest in protecting their environment, and would be motivated to organise beach clean-ups, birdwatching trips and other activities that will preserve, protect and enhance the shore, without generally incurring any cost to Council. If anyone were to potentially harm our environment, this would be rapidly detected, reported and mitigated - which cannot happen reliably if access continues to be blocked. There are several anecdotal examples of chemical spills, dead birds, pipe overflows, even boat beachings that have been detected and reported by members of the public accessing the beach. Yet our environment is healthy and beautiful - this stretch of shore is enjoyed by many species of birds, including waders, divers, gulls, egrets, heron, merganser, geese. And then there are the seals - you can frequently see them either swimming in the water off the Green Island or lounging on the rocks off the point at Seapark. Humans and wildlife can and should co-exist. 
Since the Pandemic we have all recognised the importance of coming together as a community to get to know each other, to help each other, to make Greenisland somewhere we can be proud of, whether we live on the Shore Road, the Top Road or off the Station Road. The beach is a perfect place for people to see each other in a relaxed, informal surrounding, and really appreciate where we live. Access to the beach also helps us look out for our neighbours, and considerably reduce the risks of crime or antisocial behaviour. But it's more than that. The Green Island gives our village its name - it is by definition an indispensable part of our townscape character, and the shore should be accessible to everyone. 
There is also the issue of basic rights. This beach is owned by the Council between the high and low mean tidal reaches from Shorelands to Kilroot. We the residents are ratepayers to Mid & East Antrim Borough Council. It is a violation both of our rights under the Access to the Countryside Order 1983.

Subject: Objection to Proposed Public Access Opening at Jointure Bay 
Dear Sir/Madam, 
I am writing to formally register my objection to the proposed opening of Jointure Bay for public access, which I understand is currently under consideration by Mid and East Antrim Borough Council. 
While I recognise the value of coastal access and recreational opportunities for the wider community, I believe this specific proposal raises a number of serious concerns that must be addressed: 
Environmental Impact 
Jointure Bay is an ecologically sensitive area that supports diverse marine and bird life. Increased footfall and public activity may result in habitat degradation, littering, and disturbance to local wildlife. To my knowledge, no adequate Environmental Impact Assessment has been published to support such a development. 
Safety and Infrastructure
There is a notable absence of safe and suitable infrastructure to support public access, including proper walkways, signage, and emergency response provisions.  
The rugged and secluded nature of the bay poses a risk of injury, especially to children or vulnerable visitors.  Furthermore, this area is tidal, and the tide comes in extremely quickly. There have been numerous instances of people becoming stranded on the outcrop locally known as the “Greenisland.” This creates a significant health and safety hazard for both children and adults, especially those unfamiliar with the tidal schedule. Without dedicated monitoring, warning systems, or rescue infrastructure, this risk will only increase with higher footfall  
Traffic and Parking Concerns
There are limited parking facilities nearby, which may lead to congestion, illegal parking, and inconvenience for local residents. 
Privacy and Property Boundaries 
Opening access at this location risks infringing on the privacy of adjacent properties and may encourage trespassing or anti-social behaviour in what is currently a quiet and privately enjoyed coastal space. 
Lack of Public Consultation 
Many local residents, myself included, were unaware of this proposal until recently. I believe that any such initiative should be subject to a more extensive public consultation process, with direct engagement from those most affected. 
For these reasons, I respectfully urge the council to reconsider this proposal and to prioritise the protection of our local environment, residents’ well-being, and responsible coastal management.

NI Water Pumping Station: Access to the pumping station for NI Water vehicles and personnel would be hindered by the proposed public access as the only way to gain vehicular access is to reverse in or out as there is no facility to turn vehicles at the pumping station, this will pose an adverse threat to the public. 
Health and Safety issues:  Providing public access at the proposed location will mean that the lowest point of access to the beach will always be tidal necessitating the public to walk over wet concrete / stones before accessing the sand.  It must be noted that the proposed access will necessitate the public transversing the storm drain outfall adjacent to the path which is a major health and safety issue due to extremely strong flow following heavy rain.
